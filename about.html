Stewardship Pricing: A New Asset Class for Climate Finance

By Iván Tamayo Ramos · ~1,700 words · Reading time 7 min

Read in Spanish: Mayordomía ecológica

Don't price the ecosystem. Price the stewardship of its ecological function.

The story sustainable finance keeps telling itself is that we have learned how to put a number on nature. We have not. We have learned how to put a number on the idea of nature, which is not the same thing, and the difference is now showing up in the price.

For two decades, Payments for Ecosystem Services (PES) and their younger cousin, the voluntary carbon market, have tried to convert living systems — a wetland, a watershed, a coral reef — into priced units of "natural capital." The discipline has its serious literature. The Economics of Ecosystems and Biodiversity (TEEB, 2010) and the UN's System of Environmental-Economic Accounting (SEEA-EA, 2021) have given us a rigorous vocabulary for valuing flows of ecological services. None of that is the problem. The problem is what happens when those valuations meet a project finance term sheet.

A project finance term sheet does not buy "value." It buys a contractual right to a future cash flow, backed by a defined obligation, by a counterparty whose default behaviour can be modelled. A figure that says "this mangrove provides USD 12,500 per hectare per year in coastal protection benefits" is not a cash flow. It is an accounting estimate of a public good. No senior lender writes against it. No green-bond investor accepts it as a use-of-proceeds. So the projects that need real money — coastal defense, watershed restoration, riparian corridors, restored estuaries, soil-carbon farmland — keep being routed through grants, philanthropy, and the long-suffering balance sheets of municipal governments. The asset class does not exist because the priced object is wrong.

It is not nature that should be priced. It is the stewardship of nature — the human labour, the CapEx, the OpEx of keeping ecological function healthy enough to deliver the services everyone wants to count.

The reframe

Consider a single mangrove hectare on the Pacific coast of Mexico. Its ecological function — carbon sequestration, shoreline buffering, nursery habitat for fisheries — is real, and the science is reasonably settled. But what is bankable is not the function. What is bankable is the recurring, contractual, auditable work of preserving that function: the patrols against illegal cutting, the replanting after storm damage, the salinity monitoring, the community payroll, the equipment depreciation, the management plan revisions. That is a cash-flow object. It has CapEx (initial restoration), OpEx (ongoing stewardship), an operating term (twenty, thirty, fifty years), a counterparty (a stewardship vehicle), and a buyer (a sovereign, a sub-sovereign, a corporate offtaker, or — in blended structures — a syndicate of all three).

This is not a semantic shift. It is a financial-engineering shift. Sustainability without a financial mechanism behind it is decoration. Stewardship pricing names the mechanism.

The instrument

What does the instrument look like in practice? Think of it as the marriage of two existing structures that infrastructure finance already understands: an availability-payment concession (familiar from toll roads under the Public-Private Partnership wave of the 2000s) and an energy-services-company (ESCO) contract (familiar from building retrofits).

A stewardship concession would have:

  • A defined ecological asset — the wetland, the corridor, the watershed segment — with a baseline condition documented to the level required by IFC Performance Standard 6 on Biodiversity Conservation and the IUCN Global Standard for Nature-based Solutions.
  • A long-term contract — twenty to fifty years — between a public concedente (a national environmental ministry, a state water authority, a municipal government) and a stewardship SPV.
  • An availability payment — paid quarterly, indexed, contingent on a defined set of ecological KPIs (vegetation cover, water quality, species presence, sediment retention) being met.
  • A penalty regime — payment reductions proportional to KPI underperformance, modelled on the liquidated-damages regimes of road and rail concessions.
  • A senior-debt-eligible cash flow — which is the entire point.

The stewardship SPV does what every other infrastructure SPV does. It runs a balance sheet. It raises senior debt against the availability-payment stream. It pays for the actual ecological work: salaries, vehicles, monitoring sensors, restoration CapEx, community contracts. It reports on KPIs. It gets refinanced. It is audited. It is boring, which is what bankability requires.

The capital stack

Stewardship cash flows are not commercial on day one. The early years carry restoration risk (will the ecosystem actually recover?) and counterparty risk (will the sovereign actually pay over a 30-year horizon?). This is where blended finance does what it does best — not by subsidizing the steady-state economics, but by absorbing the early-stage risk that the commercial market cannot price yet.

A defensible capital stack for a first-generation stewardship asset looks something like this:

  • Catalytic grants from a climate fund — the Green Climate Fund's project preparation facility, CAF's BiodiverCiudades programme, or the IUCN NbS Finance Accelerator — covering the upstream design, baseline studies, and the institutional setup of the SPV.
  • Concessional debt from a development finance institution — IDB Invest, IFC, EIB — covering the restoration CapEx, with a tenor longer than commercial debt would tolerate and pricing below market.
  • Senior debt from commercial lenders or institutional investors — issued as a green or sustainability-linked bond aligned with the ICMA Green Bond Principles and, where the geography supports it, the EU Taxonomy — against the availability-payment cash flow.
  • Equity from a sponsor consortium that includes a stewardship operator (a conservation NGO or specialised ecological-services firm) and an infrastructure investor with project-finance discipline.
  • In-kind contributions from local communities and adjacent landowners — recognised contractually as risk-mitigants, not as romantic externalities.

The point of the stack is not the elegance of the diagram. The point is that every tranche has a named instrument, a named source, and a named eligibility criterion. A capital structure made of generic categories is wishful thinking. A capital structure made of named instruments is a deliverable.

Three scales

The instrument matters at three scales simultaneously, and a stewardship asset that only works at one of them is not yet bankable.

At the building scale, stewardship pricing changes how we account for green roofs, urban tree canopy, and constructed wetlands attached to specific developments. Today they are CapEx items written off the project pro forma; in a stewardship-pricing regime they become OpEx revenue lines, paid by the municipal stormwater utility for the avoided grey-infrastructure cost.

At the territory scale — the regional corridor, the watershed, the historic city centre — stewardship pricing is what makes nature-based water-treatment plants, riparian buffers, and urban green corridors competitive with their grey-infrastructure equivalents in P3 procurement. This is the work being explored within the inaugural 2024 cohort of the Norman Foster Institute on Sustainable Cities, co-directed by Norman Foster and Kent Larson of the MIT Media Lab, where year-long studios on European pilot cities including the Republic of San Marino have tested how conservation and development logic can be designed into a single capital plan rather than negotiated against each other after the fact.

At the capital-stack scale — and this is the scale that decides — stewardship pricing converts a previously concessional asset into one that can carry senior debt. That is what unlocks the volume. Bloomberg CityLab has been one of the few global forums where mayors, philanthropists, and infrastructure investors put this conversation in the same room (Madrid 2026, Mexico City 2024, Amsterdam 2022); the policy infrastructure exists. The financial product does not, yet.

What this means for infrastructure today

The implication for infrastructure practitioners is operational. A P3 corridor — a highway, an urban rail line, a desalination plant — currently treats its ecological footprint as a compliance line item: an environmental and social management plan, a biodiversity offset, a mitigation budget. In a stewardship-pricing regime, the corridor's ecological scope becomes a revenue-eligible component of the concession itself, paid for through the same availability-payment mechanism that pays for the asphalt.

Work being done within sustainability departments of major infrastructure groups — including the contributions I make as Regenerative Design Consultant within ACCIONA Corporate Sustainability, across publicly announced tenders such as the SR400 Express Lanes P3 in Atlanta (delivered by the SR 400 Peach Partners consortium of ACCIONA, ACS Infrastructure, and Meridiam) and the Logan and Gold Coast Faster Rail Project in Queensland (with CPB Contractors, UGL, SMEC, and WSP, in preparation for the Brisbane 2032 Olympic and Paralympic Games) — is, in part, an effort to specify how a stewardship sub-concession could sit inside a conventional DBFOM contract without disturbing the senior-lender's appetite. The technical detail is not glamorous. It is what the asset class will live or die on.

Why now

Two trends collide in 2026. The first is the regulatory wave: the EU Taxonomy, the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), and the Taskforce on Nature-related Financial Disclosures (TNFD) now require corporates and financial institutions to disclose nature-related risks and dependencies, not just emissions. The second is the supply-side imperative: the climate and biodiversity targets of the Glasgow and Kunming-Montreal accords cannot be financed out of concessional balance sheets. The arithmetic is not close.

Stewardship pricing is one of the few mechanisms that, if it crystallises, would let mainstream capital fund the work — not through goodwill, but through cash flow. From rammed-earth community centres in northwest Mexico to Public-Private Partnership corridors across three continents, the project of regenerative design has always been the same problem at different scales. Stewardship pricing makes that problem bankable. Regenerative is "net positive," not "less bad," and net positive is what stewardship cash flows are designed to deliver.

The asset class is not built yet. Most of the work is on the legal-engineering side: model concession agreements, KPI verification protocols, default and step-in provisions, second-party-opinion methodologies for the green-bond layer. That work will accelerate over the next eighteen months, and the first generation of stewardship concessions will close before the end of the decade. The infrastructure community can either help write that contract template now or buy a worse version of it later. Those are the two options on the table.
